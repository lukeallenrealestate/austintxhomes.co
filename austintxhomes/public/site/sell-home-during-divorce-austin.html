Sell Your Austin Home
During Divorce

Selling the marital home during a divorce in Austin is more complex than a standard sale — both spouses must agree or the court must order it, Texas community property rules apply, and the transaction has to align with your divorce decree timeline. Here is everything you need to know.

Your Options

Three Paths for the
Marital Home in Divorce

Option A
Both Spouses Sell

List the home, split the equity at closing per the divorce agreement. Requires both spouses to agree on list price, price reductions, and the final sale. The most common outcome for Austin divorcing couples. I manage the transaction as a neutral agent — both parties informed, both attorneys coordinated.

Option B
One Spouse Buys Out the Other

One spouse keeps the home, compensates the departing spouse for their equity share, and refinances the mortgage into their own name. Requires a current market valuation (CMA), lender approval of the refinance, and a buyout agreement documented in the divorce decree. Common in Austin where homes have appreciated significantly.

Option C
Court-Ordered Sale

When spouses cannot agree, either party can petition the Travis County District Court to order the home sold. The court sets the parameters — pricing process, timeline, and often the listing agent. Both spouses are still entitled to their equity share. A contested sale managed professionally and on the court's timeline.

Pricing the Home: How Both Spouses Agree

The most common sticking point in Austin divorce home sales is the list price. One spouse wants to price high and wait; the other wants to sell quickly. Here's how the best transactions handle it:

  • I provide a detailed CMA — current market value with comparable sales, showing a realistic price range. Both parties and attorneys receive the same document.
  • The listing agreement includes a pre-agreed price reduction schedule — if no offer by Day X, price drops to $Y. Both spouses approve this before signing, eliminating future disputes.
  • Offer decisions are communicated to both parties simultaneously — no information asymmetry.
  • Any price reductions or counter-offer decisions require both spouses' approval (or the court's, in a court-ordered sale) before proceeding.

Spouse Buyouts

When One Spouse Keeps
the Austin Home

Austin home values have appreciated substantially over the past decade. In many divorces, one spouse wants to keep the home — for continuity, school district, or because they've built significant equity. Here's how the buyout process works.

Buyout Process — Step by Step

  • Step 1 — Establish Market Value: I provide a current CMA (or the parties agree to use a formal appraisal) to establish the home's fair market value. This is the number both attorneys will use.
  • Step 2 — Calculate Equity: Market value minus mortgage payoff, any outstanding liens or home equity loans. The equity is what's being divided.
  • Step 3 — Refinance the Mortgage: The buying spouse applies for a new mortgage in their name alone. Lender requirements: typically 6–12 months of documented income, sufficient credit, and enough equity to qualify. The refinance removes the departing spouse from the mortgage obligation.
  • Step 4 — Pay the Departing Spouse: The departing spouse receives their equity share — either from the refinance proceeds, from other marital assets traded in the settlement, or via a cash payment at closing.
  • Step 5 — Transfer Title: The departing spouse signs a Special Warranty Deed transferring their ownership interest to the staying spouse. Typically handled by the title company in coordination with both attorneys.

Buyout Considerations

Can the Buying Spouse Qualify?

This is the primary question. Lenders require income sufficient to service the new mortgage alone — with child support or alimony included only if documented for 6+ months. A buying spouse whose income was combined with their spouse's may not qualify for the same mortgage amount alone. Know this before committing to a buyout path.

Is the Buyout Price Fair?

Both spouses need an independent CMA — not one spouse's number. I provide a neutral valuation used by both attorneys. If there's significant disagreement, a formal USPAP-compliant appraisal by a licensed appraiser can resolve it definitively for the court.

Tax Implications

The transfer of the home between spouses incident to divorce is generally not a taxable event under IRC §1041. However, the capital gains basis and exclusion implications for the buying spouse's eventual future sale are important. Consult your CPA — this is not a real estate question.

Court-Ordered Sales

When the Travis County Court
Orders the Home Sold

How a Court-Ordered Sale Works in Travis County

When divorcing spouses cannot agree on the marital home — one refuses to sell, they can't agree on price, or one is blocking the transaction — either party can petition the Travis County District Court (338th, 345th, 353rd, or 419th Family District Courts) for an order compelling the sale.

The court has broad authority under Texas Family Code §7.001 to divide community property "in a manner the court deems just and right." This can include ordering the home listed for sale, setting a pricing process (requiring a CMA or appraisal as the basis), establishing a price reduction timeline, and appointing a receiver or designating the listing agent if the parties cannot agree on one.

In practice: most court-ordered sales proceed with a jointly-agreed-upon listing agent under parameters the attorneys negotiate. The court order provides the legal authority that allows the transaction to move even without both spouses' affirmative cooperation at each step.

Working a Court-Ordered Sale

Court-ordered sales require more coordination — not less professionalism. Here's what the process looks like when I'm involved:

  • Written updates to both attorneys at every milestone — listing agreement, first showing, offer received, contract executed, inspection completion, closing date confirmed.
  • Offer submissions sent simultaneously to both attorneys before any acceptance or counter.
  • Price reduction decisions per the court-approved schedule — no unilateral price changes.
  • Both spouses may attend closing at separate times (the title company accommodates separate signing appointments).
  • Equity disbursement per the court decree — separate disbursements to separate accounts.
