Section 1

What HOME Is

HOME stands for Home Options for Middle-income Empowerment. It is a two-phase Austin land development code reform passed by City Council in 2023 and 2024 that allows higher-density residential development on parcels previously restricted to single-family use. The intent: address Austin's housing supply shortage by enabling small-scale infill builders to produce more units per parent lot without changing the visual character of established neighborhoods.

Phase 1 went into effect late 2023. It allows up to 3 units on what were single-family parcels across most of Austin's residential zoning districts.

Phase 2 went into effect in 2024. It reduced minimum lot size to 1,800 sqft (down from 5,750 sqft in most SF zones), introduced Site Plan Lite for 4-16 unit projects, and Infill Plat for streamlined small-lot subdivision.

Together, the two phases changed the Austin development math on thousands of central parcels. Parcels that did not pencil as single-family teardown-and-rebuild often pencil as 3-6 unit small-lot product. Parcels that did not pencil as 4-unit infill often pencil under Site Plan Lite. The data is showing up in Q1 2026 permit numbers: ~870 HOME-eligible new permits in central Austin in a single quarter, against zero in the equivalent quarter pre-HOME.

This guide is the working reference for builders and developers evaluating HOME projects. It is not legal advice and it does not replace zoning verification with the City of Austin Development Services Department. Final eligibility determination always runs through DSD pre-development review.

Section 2

Phase 1 Mechanics

Phase 1 is the simpler of the two phases and the more accessible entry point for builders new to HOME. The core rule: parcels zoned SF-1 through SF-6 within Austin city limits can be developed with up to 3 units, regardless of original lot size, with each unit subject to the parcel's underlying zoning building envelope (height, FAR, setbacks, impervious cover).

Common Phase 1 configurations

  • Primary + 2 ADUs - The simplest path. Build the primary structure to underlying zoning, add 2 detached or attached ADUs. Often used by builders converting existing single-family inventory to add density without full demolition.
  • Duplex + ADU - Replace existing single-family with a duplex (2 attached units) plus 1 detached ADU. Typical for teardown-and-rebuild on central parcels with sufficient yard for ADU placement.
  • Triplex (3 attached units) - All 3 units in a single attached structure. Most efficient on smaller parent lots where ADU placement is constrained. Common in 78702 and 78745.
  • 3 detached small-house units - Three independent structures, each on the original parent lot (no subdivision). Less common because per-unit construction cost runs higher than attached configurations.

Phase 1 building envelope rules

Each Phase 1 unit must independently meet the parent lot's underlying zoning rules for height, FAR, setbacks, and impervious cover. The 3-unit allowance does not multiply the parent lot's building envelope - it allows up to 3 units within the existing envelope. This is the biggest source of Phase 1 misconceptions: builders sometimes assume 3 units means 3x the parent's allowable square footage, which is incorrect.

Phase 1 parking

Parking requirements were reduced under HOME but not eliminated. Most Phase 1 projects require 1 parking space per unit (down from 2 in many SF zones pre-HOME). Some transit corridors and walkable areas have zero-parking minimums under separate code provisions. Verify per parcel.

Section 3

Phase 2 Mechanics

Phase 2 is the bigger structural change. It enables small-lot subdivision through the Infill Plat process and unlocks 4-16 unit infill projects through Site Plan Lite. The core change: minimum lot size dropped from 5,750 sqft (typical SF-3) to 1,800 sqft. This is the rule that makes 78704 small-lot product economically viable.

Phase 2 small-lot subdivision math

A standard 7,500 sqft 78704 parent lot under Phase 2 can be subdivided into 3-4 small lots, each with its own structure, each sold separately. The math example:

  • Original parent lot: 7,500 sqft, single-family product with $1.4M list price typical
  • Phase 2 subdivision into 4 small lots of 1,875 sqft each
  • Each small lot supports a 1,200-1,500 sqft 2-story structure
  • Each unit lists at $675K-$795K typical, total project gross $2.7M-$3.18M
  • Net to developer typically 40-60% higher than single-family teardown-and-rebuild on the same parcel

The math holds when the parent lot is in a submarket where the small-lot product price band intersects qualified buyer demand. 78704, 78745, 78702, and 78722 are the strongest current submarkets. Outer SF-3 and SF-4 zones (78745 south of Stassney, parts of 78753) often do not pencil because the small-lot product price ceiling is too low against per-unit construction cost.

Phase 2 frontage and access

Each subdivided small lot must have legal access to a public right-of-way. This is the binding constraint on many parent lots: a 7,500 sqft parent with only 50 ft of frontage can only support 2-3 small lots accessible from the front street, even though the area math allows 4. The remaining lots require a flag-lot configuration or a private access easement, both of which add cost and complexity. Pre-vet frontage as part of acquisition due diligence.

Section 4

Lot Eligibility

Not every Austin parcel qualifies for HOME. The eligibility filter runs through five gates:

GateWhat blocks eligibility
ZoningNon-SF zoning (commercial, industrial, certain mixed-use), some MF zones with separate density caps
Historic / overlayLocal Historic District, National Register Historic District (in some cases), Conservation Combining (CC) overlay zones, Capitol View Corridor
Deed restrictionsHOA restrictions or recorded covenants that supersede zoning - common in older planned subdivisions and master-planned neighborhoods
InfrastructureInadequate water, sewer, or street capacity per City of Austin DSD pre-development review
Floodplain / environmentalFEMA 100-year floodplain, Edwards Aquifer recharge zone restrictions, critical environmental features (CEFs)
Pre-vet before pro forma Run the eligibility filter on every prospective acquisition before underwriting. A parent lot that fails one of the five gates is not a HOME deal regardless of how attractive the underlying math looks. Off-market HOME-eligible parcel sourcing filters lots through this exact gate before presenting to developers.

How to verify eligibility

  1. Pull the parcel zoning from City of Austin DSD records or AustinTexas.gov property profile
  2. Cross-reference against historic district maps and Conservation Combining overlay maps
  3. Check title commitment for recorded deed restrictions and HOA documents
  4. Submit pre-development inquiry to DSD for infrastructure capacity and zoning interpretation
  5. Engage a Texas-licensed civil engineer to verify floodplain and environmental constraints
Section 5

Site Plan Lite

Site Plan Lite is the streamlined site plan review process for HOME-Phase-2 projects of 4-16 units. It is the mechanism that makes mid-scale infill economically viable on Austin timelines.

Site Plan Lite vs traditional commercial site plan

DimensionTraditional / Lite
Review timeline6-12 months / 60-120 days
Submittal complexityFull commercial site plan / Residential-style submittal
Engineering scopeFull civil + traffic study / Reduced scope
Drainage requirementsCommercial detention / Residential-equivalent
Cost to developer$80K-$200K+ / $25K-$60K typical

The 6-9 month timeline compression is often the difference between a viable project and a project that does not pencil under construction loan interest assumptions. A 16-unit infill project that takes 6 months less to entitle saves the developer 6 months of land carry plus accelerated revenue recognition by 6 months on the back end.

Site Plan Lite eligibility

  • 4-16 residential units total
  • HOME-Phase-2-eligible parent parcel (passes the 5-gate eligibility filter above)
  • Project meets simplified drainage, parking, and access standards
  • No mixed-use or commercial component (residential-only)
  • Total impervious cover within underlying zoning maximum
Section 6

Infill Plat

Infill Plat is the streamlined subdivision plat process for HOME-Phase-2 small-lot subdivision. Where Site Plan Lite handles 4-16 unit infill projects, Infill Plat handles the lot-subdivision side - the legal mechanism that creates the small lots that Phase 2 product is built on.

Infill Plat vs traditional subdivision plat

DimensionTraditional / Infill Plat
Review timeline6-9 months / 60-90 days
Submittal scopeFull subdivision package / Streamlined residential
Public hearingOften required / Typically administrative
Engineering / surveying cost$30K-$80K / $12K-$25K typical
Use caseNew subdivisions of larger tracts / Small-lot subdivision of existing parcels

Infill Plat is what makes the 78704 small-lot subdivision math work on a 60-90 day timeline. Combined with Site Plan Lite for the building side, a HOME-Phase-2 infill project can move from acquisition to permit issuance in 4-6 months total - against 12-18 months under the pre-HOME process. This is the structural change that has unlocked Austin small-lot development.

Pro Forma Framework

The HOME pro forma framework that has held up across Q1 2026 closings:

Acquisition

  • Parent lot price - typically $400K-$650K for a HOME-eligible 7,500 sqft 78704 parcel
  • Closing costs and title insurance - 1-2% of purchase
  • Pre-development costs (zoning verification, environmental, civil) - $8K-$15K

Entitlement

  • Site Plan Lite + Infill Plat - $35K-$80K combined for engineering, surveying, fees
  • Permit fees - $8K-$25K depending on unit count
  • Land carry during entitlement - 4-6 months typical

Construction

  • Hard cost - $185-$240/sqft typical for HOME-Phase-2 small-lot product (lower than custom build, similar to spec)
  • Soft cost - $30K-$50K per unit typical
  • Construction loan interest - capitalized through completion

Marketing and listing

  • Self-marketed: $14K-$22K per unit + extended absorption window
  • MLS-listed via broker partnership: 5-6% total commission, faster absorption, higher closed price typical

Sale

  • List price per unit: $385-$520/sqft current band (Q1 2026 78704 small-lot data)
  • Closing timeline: 30-45 days post-contract
  • DOM (MLS-listed): 60-100 days median

Section 9

Listing Strategy

HOME-Phase-2 product has a unique listing strategy compared to traditional new construction. The buyer pool skews toward design-conscious central Austin buyers (often paying for the location plus the new construction together) rather than commodity production-builder buyers in suburban submarkets.

What HOME product needs from a listing partner

  • MLS launch with full marketing stack - Photography that captures the small-lot design language. Drone shots that show site plan integration with the surrounding neighborhood. Video walk-through that handles the multi-unit layout intuitively.
  • Buyer-agent network access - Most central Austin buyers are represented by exclusive buyer agents. The MLS-launch advantage is structurally larger for HOME product because the buyer pool concentrates with represented buyers more than commodity new construction buyer pools.
  • HOME mechanic fluency in showings - Buyer questions on HOME product run different from traditional questions. What is the lot configuration. Is there a shared driveway. What is the regime structure. A listing agent who can answer these in showings without deferring to the builder accelerates contract velocity.
  • Pricing discipline and adjustment cadence - HOME pricing is still in discovery. Listing partners with current submarket comp data and a disciplined price-adjustment cadence move product at higher net than partners who anchor to launch price and let DOM stretch.
  • Condo regime coordination - Many HOME projects close as condo regimes for legal lot structuring. The listing partner needs to understand regime mechanics, CCRs, and HOA setup. Condo regime sales reference.

The listing partnership conversation for HOME builders is structurally different from the conversation for production-builder spec product. The math favors broker partnership more strongly because the absorption gap is wider and the buyer-agent network access advantage is larger.

Section 10

Common Builder Mistakes

Patterns observed across the first 18 months of HOME-Phase-2 closings that cost builders money:

1. Skipping pre-development eligibility verification

Builders who submit Site Plan Lite or Infill Plat applications without first verifying parent lot eligibility through DSD pre-development review hit a 30-40% rejection rate. Each rejection adds 60-90 days plus resubmittal cost. The pre-development inquiry is $1,500-$3,000 and 30-45 days. It pays for itself.

2. Underwriting parking incorrectly

Reduced parking requirements vary by submarket, transit corridor proximity, and individual zoning interpretation. Underwriting a project at 1 space per unit when the actual requirement runs at 1.5 typical can force a unit count reduction or a redesign mid-project. Verify per parcel, not by general HOME rule.

3. Designing units that miss the buyer pool

The 78704 small-lot buyer pool wants 2-bed/2.5-bath, 1,200-1,500 sqft, 2-story, with at least one outdoor space. Builders who design 1-bed/1-bath 800 sqft units to maximize per-parcel unit count discover the price ceiling is not where they assumed. Smaller is not always better - the buyer pool has minimum-viable-product preferences and pricing reflects them.

4. Self-marketing slow absorption

Self-marketing HOME product extends DOM by 30-60% versus MLS-listed. The carry cost on extended DOM typically exceeds the saved commission. Run the math on the specific project before committing to self-marketing as a savings strategy.

5. Late condo regime setup

HOME projects that close as condo regimes need the regime structure (CCRs, HOA, declaration of condominium) finalized before MLS launch. Builders who scramble on regime setup mid-listing lose 30-60 days and create transaction-certainty problems with represented buyers. Set up the regime during entitlement, not during marketing.

HOME Initiative FAQ

What is the Austin HOME initiative?

HOME stands for Home Options for Middle-income Empowerment. It is a two-phase Austin land development code reform passed in 2023-2024 that allows higher-density residential development on parcels previously restricted to single-family use. Phase 1 (effective late 2023) allows up to 3 units on what were single-family lots. Phase 2 (effective 2024) reduced minimum lot size to 1,800 sqft, introduced Site Plan Lite for 4-16 unit projects, and Infill Plat for streamlined small-lot subdivision.

What lots qualify for HOME Phase 1?

Most parcels zoned SF-1 through SF-6 within Austin city limits qualify for HOME Phase 1, with exceptions for historic districts, certain overlay zones, and parcels with deed restrictions that supersede zoning. Phase 1 allows up to 3 units regardless of original lot size, with each unit capped at the parcel's underlying zoning building envelope. Pre-vetting eligibility is the first step before any pro forma.

What lots qualify for HOME Phase 2 small-lot subdivision?

HOME Phase 2 allows lot subdivision down to 1,800 sqft minimum lot size in eligible zoning districts. The original parent lot must be subdividable through Infill Plat (a streamlined process that bypasses traditional subdivision review for qualifying projects). Eligibility depends on parent lot size, frontage, infrastructure capacity, and zoning. A 7,500 sqft parent lot can typically be split into 3-4 small-lot units under Phase 2.

What is Site Plan Lite?

Site Plan Lite is a streamlined site plan review process introduced under HOME Phase 2 for projects of 4-16 units. It bypasses the standard commercial site plan review timeline (typically 6-12 months) in favor of a residential-style review (typically 60-120 days) when the project meets HOME-Phase-2 criteria. Site Plan Lite is the mechanism that makes 4-16 unit infill projects economically viable on shorter timelines.

What is Infill Plat?

Infill Plat is a streamlined subdivision plat process introduced under HOME Phase 2 specifically for small-lot subdivision of existing single-family parcels. It replaces traditional subdivision plat review (typically 6-9 months) with a faster process (typically 60-90 days) when the project meets Infill Plat criteria. Infill Plat is the mechanism that makes 1,800-sqft small-lot subdivision economically viable.

What is the typical price-per-square-foot for HOME-Phase-2 product in central Austin?

HOME-Phase-2 small-lot product in 78704 and 78745 has settled at $385-$520 per square foot in Q1 2026 closing data. This is below traditional 78704 single-family ($445-$580/sqft) but reflects the smaller-lot, smaller-house formula. Pricing is still in early discovery as the first wave of HOME projects close - the ceiling will rise as the buyer pool absorbs the value proposition.

How long does HOME-Phase-2 product take to absorb?

Q1 2026 data shows HOME-Phase-2 product absorbing in 4-7 months when listed via MLS partnership. Self-marketed product runs longer (8-12 months) because the buyer pool for small-lot product skews toward buyer-agent represented buyers - exactly the network MLS launch unlocks. The absorption gap is wider for HOME product than for traditional single-family.

Can ADUs be added under HOME Phase 1?

Phase 1's 3-unit allowance can include a primary structure plus 2 ADUs, or 3 attached/detached units configured as a duplex plus ADU, or other compliant arrangements. Each unit must meet building code, fire code, and parking requirements per the underlying zoning. ADU configuration is often the simplest Phase 1 path for builders with existing single-family inventory looking to add density without full demolition.

Does HOME apply to lots in historic districts?

Most lots in designated historic districts and Conservation Combining (CC) overlay zones do not qualify for HOME density bonuses. Historic-district lots remain subject to Historic Landmark Commission review and the underlying historic preservation rules. Some CC zones permit limited HOME application but typically require commission approval. Pre-vetting historic and overlay status is essential before any HOME pro forma.

How is HOME affecting Austin neighborhoods?

Q1 2026 permit data shows ~870 HOME-eligible new permits in central Austin, concentrated in 78704, 78702, 78745, and 78722. The visible neighborhood pattern: replacement of older single-family homes (typically 1950s-1970s vintage) with 3-6 unit small-lot product on the same parent footprint. Neighborhoods near transit corridors and walkable amenities are seeing the highest HOME activity. Surrounding home values have shown stable to positive trends, contradicting concerns about density-driven price drops.
