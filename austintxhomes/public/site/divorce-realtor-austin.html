Texas Community Property
& Your Marital Home

Texas Is a Community Property State — Here's What That Means

Texas is one of only nine community property states in the US. Under Texas Family Code §3.002, property acquired by either spouse during the marriage is presumed to be community property — owned equally by both spouses, regardless of whose name is on the deed, title, or mortgage.

For the marital home, this means: both spouses have a legal ownership interest. Neither can sell the home, encumber it with new debt, or give it away without the other's consent — unless a court authorizes it. Both spouses must sign the listing agreement and the closing documents.

The exception is separate property — a home owned by one spouse before the marriage, or received as a gift or inheritance during the marriage, may be separate property. But even separate property homes can become complicated if marital funds paid the mortgage or made improvements. Texas courts apply the inception of title rule to determine ownership.

Working with an Austin divorce attorney alongside an experienced real estate agent is essential for navigating these property classification questions before listing.

Texas Divorce & Real Estate — Key Facts
⚖️ Community property state. Property acquired during marriage is presumed 50/50 — regardless of whose name is on the deed.
✍️ Both spouses must sign. Listing agreement, purchase contract, and closing documents all require both signatures — or a court order.
⏱️ 60-day waiting period. Texas requires 60 days from filing before a divorce can be granted — but you can list and sell the home before the divorce is finalized.
🏛️ Travis County District Court. Austin divorces are handled in Travis County. The court can order the home sold, set a pricing process, or appoint a receiver if needed.
💰 Equity to both at closing. When the home sells, both spouses receive their equity share via separate disbursements at closing per the divorce decree or agreement.
🏠 Homestead exemption ends. After the divorce, only one spouse's homestead exemption can be claimed on the Austin property. File the correct exemption for your new home after January 1.
📋 Separate property. Pre-marital home, gifts, or inheritance may be separate property — but Texas courts require clear and convincing evidence to overcome the community property presumption.

In Texas, an agent can serve as an intermediary — a neutral transaction facilitator — when representing both parties in the same transaction, with written consent from both (Texas Intermediary status under TREC rules). In divorce situations, I typically function as a neutral party focused solely on maximizing the sale price and executing cleanly, with both attorneys involved throughout. In a Texas community property divorce, the marital home's equity is typically divided equally — though the court can divide it differently based on fault, children's needs, or other factors. Equity is calculated as: sale price minus mortgage payoff, closing costs, commissions, and any outstanding liens. Both spouses receive their share at closing via separate disbursements to their respective accounts. If one spouse is buying out the other, they refinance the mortgage into their name and pay the departing spouse their equity share, often via the title company.

In many cases, listing early while the divorce is in process is advantageous — it gives more time to achieve the best price and ensures both parties receive equity before the decree is entered.
